How to think before you click

Start with the business question

Good reporting starts with a clear question

Before building a chart or opening a dashboard, decide what decision you are trying to support. Clear questions lead to clearer reports, faster analysis and less rework.

Examples of useful questions
  • Which suppliers have increased in cost over the last three months?
  • Which assets have the highest maintenance cost this quarter?
  • How has spend changed by site, region or cost centre?
  • Which categories are driving our emissions trend?
  • Where are we seeing anomalies, leakage or margin pressure?

Questions to avoid

  • “Show me everything.”
  • “I’ll click around and see what happens.”
  • “I just need a dashboard with lots of charts.”
Why vague requests create poor reports

Without a clear question, it is easy to overbuild dashboards, choose the wrong metric, or produce charts that look impressive but do not help anyone make a decision.

A simple question framework

Step Prompt Example
1. Outcome What decision are we trying to make? Decide which assets need investigation
2. Metric What number or comparison matters? Total maintenance cost per asset
3. Timeframe What period are we reviewing? Current quarter vs previous quarter
4. Lens How do we want to break it down? By depot, supplier or asset type
Best practice

A good report usually answers one core question clearly. It does not need to answer every possible question at once.

Trusting what you see

Understanding curated datasets and fields

What a curated dataset is

A curated dataset is a prepared view of data that has already been structured for a business purpose. It is designed so users can explore, filter and summarise information without needing to understand the full technical model behind it.

What you should expect
  • Clear naming that reflects business meaning
  • Consistent fields across similar reports
  • Fields that are appropriate for grouping, filtering or summarising
  • Logic already applied where needed

What a curated dataset is not

  • It is not the raw source file
  • It is not a place for rebuilding complex joins from scratch
  • It is not an invitation to recreate Conifr’s deeper modelling logic
Rule of thumb

If a view already answers the business question well, use it as-is. Only ask for a new dataset when the current curated view genuinely cannot support the analysis.

How to read a dataset description

Purpose

What the dataset is intended to help you analyse.

Grain

The level of detail in each row, such as per invoice line, per asset, per day or per job.

Key dimensions

The main columns you can use to filter, group or compare, such as supplier, site, asset or month.

Field types you will commonly see

Field type What it does How users usually work with it
Date / time Shows when an event, transaction or record occurred Filter by month, quarter, year or custom range
Category Groups data into labels such as supplier, site or waste stream Use for grouping and comparison
Metric Numeric measure such as spend, quantity, cost or emissions Summarise using total, average, count or minimum/maximum
Identifier Unique reference such as invoice ID, asset ID or job number Use for traceability and drill-through, not broad trend charts

Data literacy without the technical overhead

How to understand a dataset before using it

Three checks before you start

  1. Check the purpose. Does this dataset match the question you need to answer?
  2. Check the grain. Is each row at the level you expect, such as per invoice line or per asset?
  3. Check the main fields. Do you have the dimensions and metrics you need for comparison?

Why grain matters

Many reporting errors happen because users compare or total records at the wrong level of detail. For example, a dataset at invoice-line level behaves differently from a monthly summary dataset.

Common issue

Summing a field in a detailed dataset can produce much larger totals than expected if the same business event appears across multiple rows for valid reasons.

Getting started

Building your first report

The core workflow

  1. Choose the curated dataset that best matches your question.
  2. Apply filters to narrow the records you want to analyse.
  3. Summarise the measure you care about.
  4. Group results by the category or time period you want to compare.
  5. Choose a chart or table format that makes the answer easy to read.

A simple example

Question: Which suppliers drove the most spend last month?

  • Select a supplier spend dataset
  • Filter to last month
  • Summarise total spend
  • Group by supplier
  • Sort highest to lowest
  • Use a bar chart or table

What each step is doing

Step Meaning Typical use
Filter Narrows down the records included Last 30 days, one region, one supplier type
Summarise Turns many rows into a single number or grouped totals Total spend, average cost, count of records
Group Breaks the summary into categories or time buckets By month, by supplier, by site, by asset
Visualise Changes how the answer is displayed Table, line chart, bar chart, area chart
Good habit

Build the logic first. Worry about visual styling second. A well-structured table is often the fastest way to confirm you are answering the right question before converting it into a chart.

Filtering and summarising

Using filters, summaries and groupings well

Filters

Use filters to define scope before you compare or total anything.

  • Date range
  • Status
  • Site or region
  • Supplier or category
Summaries

Use summaries to create the metric that answers your question.

  • Total
  • Average
  • Count
  • Minimum / maximum
Groupings

Use groupings to compare that metric across time or categories.

  • By month
  • By supplier
  • By asset
  • By cost centre

Strong pattern

Example: “Show total maintenance cost by month for Depot A this year.”

  1. Filter to Depot A
  2. Filter to current year
  3. Summarise total maintenance cost
  4. Group by month

Weak pattern

Example: “Show maintenance.”

  • No timeframe
  • No metric
  • No comparison
  • No clear decision use

Tips for dates and categories

Scenario Recommended approach
Trend analysis Group by month or week and keep the timeframe consistent
Comparing categories Limit the chart to the top categories so it stays readable
Investigating anomalies Start broad, then filter further using drill-through or detail views
Executive reporting Use fewer groups and clearer labels to avoid clutter

Choosing the right visual

Tables, charts and formatting

Use a table when
  • You need detail and traceability
  • You want to sort and inspect records
  • You need to export the result
Use a bar chart when
  • You are comparing categories
  • You want to show ranking
  • Labels are important
Use a line chart when
  • You are showing change over time
  • The trend matters more than exact values
  • You want to compare periods or series

Formatting settings that improve readability

  • Show currency clearly where needed
  • Reduce decimals unless precision matters
  • Use conditional formatting sparingly to highlight exceptions
  • Hide fields that do not help the reader
  • Rename technical field labels into business language when possible

A note on colour

Use colour to support the message, not to decorate the chart. Keep the palette restrained and consistent so people can read the pattern quickly.

Dashboards

Using dashboards as an operating view

What dashboards are best for

  • Tracking a small set of important metrics
  • Monitoring change over time
  • Comparing performance across categories
  • Giving teams a shared reporting view

What dashboards are not for

  • Showing every chart available
  • Replacing detailed investigation
  • Combining unrelated topics on one page

How to interact with a dashboard

Action What it helps you do
Use dashboard filters Narrow the whole view to a timeframe, location or category
Open a chart in full view Inspect the report in more detail
Drill into a data point See which underlying records make up that number
Export where appropriate Take the result into Excel or another workflow if needed
Professional dashboard rule

Keep each dashboard focused on one operational story. Four clear charts that answer one business question are more useful than twelve charts competing for attention.

Organisation and sharing

Saving, sharing and keeping reporting tidy

Personal vs shared work

Location Use it for
Personal area Drafts, tests, temporary analysis, rough exploration
Shared area Team-facing reporting, recurring views, agreed dashboards

Naming conventions that help

  • Start with the business topic, not the dataset name
  • Be specific about timeframe or metric where relevant
  • Keep titles readable for someone outside your immediate team
Examples
  • Monthly Supplier Spend by Region
  • Asset Maintenance Cost Trend
  • Waste Volume by Site and Stream

Ownership and governance

Draft reports

Owned by the person exploring a question.

Team dashboards

Should have an agreed owner who reviews relevance and accuracy.

New core reporting views

Should be discussed with Conifr if they require new logic or curated modelling.

Good reporting is also good housekeeping. Clear titles, agreed ownership and a tidy shared area make it much easier for teams to trust and reuse reporting.

Self-service boundaries

How to work safely with Conifr data

What you should do

  • Use curated datasets as your starting point
  • Apply filters, summaries and groupings to answer business questions
  • Use drill-through and detail tables when you need to investigate
  • Ask Conifr for help when you think a new curated view is needed

What to avoid

  • Rebuilding complex logic that is already handled in a curated view
  • Mixing together datasets that are not intended for the same question
  • Treating technical field names as if they always mean the same thing across every dataset
  • Assuming a strange result must be wrong before checking the grain and filters

When to self-serve vs when to ask Conifr

Situation Best path
You want a simple trend, ranking or comparison from an existing curated view Self-serve
You need a new metric that depends on business logic not currently exposed Ask Conifr
You suspect a dataset should combine multiple layers of information in a new way Ask Conifr
You want a different chart or a new filter on an existing report Usually self-serve
Why this boundary exists

Conifr adds value by curating the data model, joins and reporting logic beneath the surface. Self-service works best when users explore the curated layer rather than trying to reconstruct that deeper analytical work themselves.

Common mistakes

The most common reporting errors and how to avoid them

Mistake What it looks like How to fix it
Wrong grain Totals look too high or duplicated Check what each row represents before summarising
Missing date filter Current and historic periods are mixed together Always confirm the timeframe first
Too many categories in one chart Unreadable bar chart or crowded legend Limit to top values or split into separate views
Using average when total is needed Results feel unexpectedly low or flat Reconfirm the metric matches the business question
Unclear labels Readers do not understand what the chart shows Rename titles and axes in business language
Before escalating a “data issue”
  1. Check the timeframe
  2. Check the filters
  3. Check whether the dataset grain matches your assumption
  4. Check whether you are using the right metric
A healthy approach

Treat unexpected results as a prompt to inspect the question, filters and dataset design first. Many surprises are caused by reporting setup rather than underlying data quality issues.

Embedded AI analyst

Using Charlie to ask business questions in plain English

What Charlie is good for

  • Quick summaries and comparisons
  • Investigating cost spikes or unusual movement
  • Finding the right direction before building a full report
  • Exploring profitability, margin, volume or operational patterns

What makes a good Charlie prompt

  • State the metric clearly
  • Include the timeframe
  • Specify the comparison or dimension
  • Ask one question at a time

Example prompts

Finance and cost
  • Show total supplier spend by month for this financial year
  • Which suppliers increased in cost the most compared with last quarter?
  • What are the top ten cost categories by site this month?
Operations and performance
  • Which assets had the highest maintenance cost this quarter?
  • Show monthly fuel cost trend by depot
  • Which jobs have the largest variance between expected and actual cost?
Best use of Charlie: Use it to move quickly from a business question to an initial answer. Once you know the pattern you want to track regularly, turn it into a saved report or dashboard.

Glossary and checklist

Quick glossary and first-week checklist

Glossary

Term Meaning
Curated view A business-ready dataset designed for reporting and analysis
Grain The level of detail represented by one row
Dimension A descriptive field used to group or filter, such as site or supplier
Metric A numeric value used for analysis, such as cost, quantity or emissions
Drill-through Opening a summary result to inspect the underlying records

Your first-week checklist

  • Review the datasets relevant to your role
  • Understand the grain of the main views you use
  • Build one simple report from a curated dataset
  • Save one draft in your personal area
  • Review one shared dashboard used by your team
  • Try one plain-English question in Charlie
  • Note any recurring question that may need a new curated view
Final reminder: Use Conifr to explore trusted data confidently, but do not feel you need to do everything yourself. Strong self-service works best alongside a curated data model and access to deeper analytical support when needed.
