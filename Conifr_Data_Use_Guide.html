Finding your way

Navigating Dashboards

Dashboards are entry points

Think of a dashboard as a shop window. It is meant for quick review and monitoring. Dashboards are not your working views. If you want to dig into the data, you need to step through the window into the actual report.

Dashboard vs Report
  • Dashboard: A collection of fixed charts showing a high-level summary. Great for daily checks.
  • Report: The individual, interactive workspace where a single chart lives. This is where real analysis happens.

How to move past the dashboard

To investigate a number or change how a chart looks, you must click the title of the card on the dashboard. This opens the full report view.

This catches most users out

Many users try to do all their analysis on the dashboard screen by clicking the bars or lines directly. This only applies a temporary filter. Always click the title to open the report properly.

Controlling your data

Understanding Filters

Dashboard filters vs Report filters

Filters exist in two places, and they behave differently:

  • Dashboard Filters: Found at the top of a dashboard. They apply to multiple charts at once.
  • Report Filters: Found inside an individual report. They are the "hardcoded" rules for that specific view.

Filter persistence

Filters persist after interactions. If you apply a filter, it stays there until you remove it. Active filters are visible at the top of a report.

Crucial rule

If something looks wrong, check the filters first. Overlapping or conflicting filters are the cause of 90% of data questions.

Adding a Filter

To narrow down the data in a full report, click the Add Filter button.

Setting Timeframes

When filtering dates, use relative timeframes like "Previous Quarter" so the report automatically updates over time.

Common filter scenarios

Situation What to do
The chart is completely blank. Remove all filters and add them back one by one.
A site or supplier is missing. Open the full report, check the top filter bar, and clear exclusions.
Numbers are lower than expected. Check the top bar for active filter pills and click the 'X' to remove them.
What just happened?

You changed a dashboard filter, but one chart didn't update.

Why it happens: Dashboard filters must be manually wired to every chart. If it ignores your filter, it isn't connected.

What to do: Open the full report and apply the filter directly inside the report view.

Looking closer

Drill-Down Behaviour

This is the most common source of confusion

Understanding exactly what happens when you click on a chart element is the difference between finding the right answer and getting completely lost.

What clicking a chart actually does

When you click a bar, line, or pie slice, the system applies a hard filter for that specific value and opens a pop-up to show you the next level of detail.

  • It isolates the data.
  • It does NOT recreate the full context of your original view.

A practical example

Imagine a chart showing Total Cost by Category. You see a massive spike in "Fuel Cost" and click the bar to investigate.

What happens: The system applies a filter: Category = Fuel Cost.

What you see next: The view changes entirely. You are no longer looking at categories. The system might show you Fuel Cost by Supplier, or simply list every raw transaction. The original context is gone.

How to drill down safely

  1. Open the full report first. Never try to do deep drill-downs from the dashboard view.
  2. Identify what you want to isolate. (e.g., "I only want to look at Depot A").
  3. Apply a filter manually instead of clicking the chart. This keeps you in control of the view.
  4. Change the grouping. If you were looking at Month, change the grouping to Supplier to see who made up that month's total.
What just happened?

You clicked a bar chart, the screen changed, and now you can't get back.

Why it happens: You initiated a drill-down. The system applied a temporary filter and altered the grouping.

What to do: Look at the top of your screen for the breadcrumb navigation. Clicking the title of the original report will return you to safety.

Adapting the view

Changing Charts & Views

Tables vs Charts

Different views serve different purposes. You can switch between them using the visualisation menu in a full report.

  • Tables = Validation: Always use a table when you need to verify numbers, sort highest to lowest, or check data quality.
  • Charts = Presentation: Use charts to highlight trends, outliers, or proportions to an audience.

Adjusting a table

Sorting: Click any column header to sort ascending or descending. This is the fastest way to find top offenders.

Formatting Options

Settings Menu: Use the gear icon next to the visualization button to hide columns, format currency, or set a top-limit cutoff.

Troubleshooting

Fixing Common Issues

If this looks wrong, it usually means there is a simple structural issue with your view. Use this diagnostic table before assuming the data is broken.

Issue Fix
Numbers are massively inflated Switch to a Table view. Check if rows are duplicated. You are likely summing a metric across the wrong grain.
Missing data points A dashboard filter is acting on a field that doesn't exist. Open full report and apply filter manually.
Drill-down looks like nonsense Don't click the chart. Manually change the "Group By" field instead.

Dealing with "No Results"

Filters are too restrictive or conflict with each other. Clear all filters. Re-add the date filter first, then check the data again.

Saving & Editing Reports

Viewing vs Editing

You can change filters, sort tables, and switch visualisations as much as you like while viewing a report. None of this breaks the report for anyone else unless you hit Save.

Save vs Save As

When you click save after making changes, you will be prompted with a choice:

  • Replace (Save): Overwrites the existing report. Everyone sees your changes immediately.
  • Save as new (Duplicate): Creates a brand new copy. The safest way to experiment.
Golden Rule

Do not casually overwrite shared reports. If a report lives in a team folder, use "Save As" to make your own copy before changing metrics or fundamental groupings.

Personal vs Shared Reports

Always save drafts and tests into your personal collection.

Personal Area Your private sandbox. Safe to break and test logic.
Shared Area The source of truth. Built from curated datasets.
What just happened?

You opened a dashboard and the charts look completely different than yesterday.

Why it happens: Someone opened the underlying report, changed the visualization or grouping, and hit "Save" instead of "Save As".

What to do: Open the report, review the history (if available), or manually revert the chart back to its intended state and save it.

Putting it into practice

Example Walkthroughs

Example 1: Investigate a Cost Spike

You are looking at a dashboard showing Monthly Costs, and last month has a massive, unexpected spike.

  1. Open the report: Click the title of the card to open the full report workspace.
  2. Check filters: Note what filters are currently applied.
  3. Filter to the spike: Manually add a filter for the specific month in question.
  4. Break it down: Open the "Group By" setting. Remove "Month" and replace it with "Category" or "Supplier".
  5. Switch view: Change the visualisation to a Table, then sort the "Total Cost" header highest to lowest. The culprit will float to the top.

Example 2: Find Top Suppliers

You need to know who your top 10 suppliers were over the last quarter.

  1. Start clean: Open a curated supplier spend dataset.
  2. Filter timeframe: Set the Date to "Previous Quarter".
  3. Group the data: Click "Group By" and select "Supplier Name".
  4. Summarise: Ensure the metric is set to "Sum of Total Spend".
  5. Sort & View: View as a Table and sort descending, or use a Bar Chart with the settings gear to show only the "Top 10".

Example 3: Fix a Broken Report

You open a shared report, and it says "No Results" or looks completely broken.

  1. Reset filters: Look at the active filter pills. Remove all of them by clicking the 'X'.
  2. Switch to table: Change the visualisation back to a Table. This removes chart rendering errors.
  3. Check grouping: Ensure you aren't grouping by a field that is completely empty.
  4. Rebuild slowly: Add the date filter back first. Check the data. Then add the next filter. Find the exact step that breaks the view.

Example 4: Validate a Number

A dashboard says $50k, but your spreadsheet says $42k. Find the difference.

  1. Open the report: Click the card title.
  2. Check filters: Are you looking at exactly the same timeframe? Are exclusions matching?
  3. Compare granularly: Group by "Month" and view as a Table. Compare the monthly totals to find the specific month that differs.
  4. Drill deeper: Filter the report to only show that month. Group by "Job ID" or "Asset" to find the exact line item causing the variance.
The ultimate takeaway

When in doubt, clear your filters, switch to a table, and look at the raw rows. Curated datasets are designed to be reliable; visualisations and filters are usually where the confusion happens.
